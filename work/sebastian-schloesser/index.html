2 – Authorship

How to vary perspective in generative authorship?

Exploring technical and philosophical underpinnings of training AI models.

When contemplating the waste removal system, we observe significant lock-in. Curbs are “set in stone”, zoning laws are hard to change, and the department of sanitation has protocols and procedures to match. Just like with Lanier’s MIDI example, this is a system that works but is limited in its ability to evolve. The solution is innovation in governance. DAOs might be an interesting option, but a politico-cultural reform in our institutions might be more appropriate.

I believe an authorship system should adhere to the following crucial tenets:

  • There is a direct business model that provides proportional attribution to creators
  • The tools and platforms used in the system enshrine diverse perspectives
  • Tools need to be customizable but have a strongly designed default configuration

When third parties fuel a platform, incentives are misaligned. When consumers pay for content, and creators get paid for it, externalities are minimized. The challenge, of course, is competing with ubiquitous free content. Here I think blockchains might help provide attribution and compensation matching the complex networks of creation that we see.

Of course, AI datasets need to be trained with more balanced data or weighted appropriately since we can't really change the biases that already exist in the world and that will surface through labelers and the availability of certain types of content. However, I think we need to go deeper and actually have tools be written by a diverse team of people. Most software commonly used is created by a small and homogeneous group in Silicon Valley. It is also inevitable that systems will have biases and limitations. One thing that sets humans apart from AI is that our “training data sets” are infinitely more nuanced.

Even great diversity in the creator team cannot foresee all use cases, and that is why tools should have customizability. They should be usable out of the box and have strong design principles ingrained, unlike Emacs, which can take forever to configure satisfactorily. Simply allowing the user to customize everything is a lack of design initiative. However, if the user has a specific need and needs to alter a particular behavior, they should be able to.

Examples:

  1. Patreon, Medium
    • These platforms allow consumers to pay creators directly and simply take a cut for running the environment.

  2. Myspace, Scuttlebutt
    • These tools allow people to express themselves authentically. Myspace allowed HTML customization of your page and encouraged people to represent their personality. Scuttlebutt mimics real-life gossip information spread, which allows for a more natural distribution of knowledge, rather than the curation and pigeonholing that social media algorithms create.

  3. VSCode, Emacs, XCode
    • In the world of text editors, there are extremely different approaches. I think VSCode is great because it is open source and fully customizable, but when you download it, you can start using it and it is intuitive. Emacs is infinitely customizable but it can take a really long time to get it to a usable state, and when it breaks, you can waste hours just fixing basic functionality. XCode, on the other hand, locks you into a very dogmatic approach and doesn’t allow for almost any customizability. However, it functions quite smoothly. I think a balance is ideal here, and therefore, VSCode wins.

A Data Manifesto

Collecting data can be an immensely tedious task. Whenever possible, I believe it is essential to leverage automated, continuous collection. The data I’m using have been collected for years in the background without effort from my part. Of course, they afford a utilitarian view of myself as a commodity to be advertised to, but this doesn’t prevent the reclaiming of it for the gleaning of alternative insights.

When the hypothesis in question is too broad and complex, I believe taking an individual case perspective can help illuminate the key levers of the system. In this case, the environmental cost of online shopping vs. brick-and-mortar is impossible to calculate. The nature of this problem is collective at heart, and one person won’t influence the whole. However, we make decisions on a daily basis, that in aggregate do matter. How else can we guide our choices other than by analyzing our particular situation with the data at hand?
