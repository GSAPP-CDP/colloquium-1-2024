1 – Systems

What is the system?

Energy Supply Systems in Underdeveloped Countries

So initially in the reading I went with something that aligns with what a system is much more clearly, and I went with energy supply systems, which offer clear documented systems and structures, and decided to specify that even further where it applies to underdeveloped countries.

This system is very important because its existence is what ensures reliable and sustainable energy to poor areas, which obviously will greatly improve living standards and help with economic development.

The energy supply system has a very clear structure. First, we have energy generation, which includes power plants like solar, wind, hydro, and fossil fuels. These power plants are where energy is made. Next comes the distribution part, which involves transmission lines and local networks that carry energy to where it's needed. Then, we have the consumption part, which includes homes, businesses, and public services that use the energy. Lastly, there's maintenance, which keeps everything running well.

In this system, we can understand it as a balance of stocks and flows. Stocks are reserves of energy, like batteries and fossil fuels, and infrastructure like power plants and transmission lines. Flows are the rates at which energy is produced and used, as well as the money invested in energy infrastructure.

Feedback loops play a big role in this system. A reinforcing feedback loop happens when more energy access leads to more economic activities, making more money that can be reinvested in energy projects. This creates a positive cycle of improvement. A balancing feedback loop involves maintenance and support systems that ensure stability and stop infrastructure from breaking down, keeping the energy supply steady. But upon further understanding, this system faces several problems.

There are: Physical Constraints: Hard to reach places, tough terrain, availability of resources. Technological Constraints: Access to modern tech, reliability of networks. Financial Constraints: Limited money for projects, economic problems. Leverage Points: Investing in renewable energy to reduce fossil fuel use.

What makes this system very clear and direct, and why I used it as an example, is because its clear to follow how energy supply directly affects economic growth and quality of life. Access to energy can change communities; allowing businesses to work, students to study, and hospitals to operate effectively. The use of renewable energy can also reduce environmental damage, making the system more sustainable in the long run.

So as a conclusion: The energy supply system to poor countries is: Hierarchical Has Stocks and Flows Affected by Constraints and Needs Resilience Understanding these dynamics helps improve energy access, support sustainable growth, and better life in poor regions.

I want to have more fun with this class, so instead of going with something clear and reliable, I decided to go with something a little harder to pin down - time perception. I understand that this will come with much more critique, but I am welcome to it.

PERCEPTION OF TIME IN NY

The perception of time is the system chosen because of its fluidity between abstract and concrete, the way it exists in many more obviously tangible systems, and the effect it has on individual and societal behaviors. It is broad enough to vary depending on culture, but specific enough to be analysed through doors, paths, and apps. The perception of time can be conceptualized as a system because it has many interacting components, even though it seems the components are fluid depending on what factors are chosen to analyze. These interacting components are tied together by an application. They include environmental phenomena, biological processes, cognitive factors, and how it all appears in our tech. And the system holds its importance in the way it affects how we live our lives, it influences our decisions, our relationships, our work, and our mental health. Understanding this invisible influence will improve the way we design products, buildings, and our cities to improve wellbeing and productivity.

Inputs can be factors that influence the perception of time, especially controlled factors, such as how much daylight is accessible, the flow of people in space, sounds, temperature, etc. The outputs are cognitive responses, energy levels, human interactions, speeding up, slowing down, etc. And the resources are the hardest part to define, but in this case, they could be anything between attention, memory, money, consumption, time spent in a place, etc.

Rube Goldberg Machine

This is how we planned our Rube Goldberg sequence. First, our Rube Goldberg machine was triggered from the previous group's dancing Trump video.

  • Dance Movement Detection:
    • We used a webcam that captured the movements of the dancer, prompting him to dance. And then once the program recognizes that the subject is dancing, will trigger the IFTTT device. .
  • Hairdryer Activation:
    • When dance movements are detected, the computer sends a signal to an IFTTT plug.
    • IFTTT triggers the hairdryer to blow air onto the makeshift carousel.
  • Spinning Carousel and Water bucket:
    • The carousel rotates as soon as the hairdryer air hits it, and will spin a few times gatehring momentum. There is a string tied to it, which will eventually yank on the next object, a cup filled with water.
  • Arduino Liquid Sensor:
    • The water spills into a container, and activates the Arduino liquid sensor.
    • The Arduino sends a signal to the second computer.
  • Hand-Holding Sensor:
    • The second computer prompts people to hold two ends of Arduino jumper wires by asking them to hold hands and make a circle.
    • When the circuit is completed, the second sequence is triggered.
  • Celebratory Screen and Clap Sensor:
    • The celebratory screen displays a celebratory and invites the audience to clap.
    • The sound sensor detects the clap, triggering the next group’s machine.

    Authorship philosophy - Perception of Time in Cities

    So ultimately, the level of authorship users should have on online tools, if I’m speaking from the voice of the developer, should be minimal enough that every input is constrained, feeding into the digital tool. The output of said digital tool will reflect only some of the user input, but will mostly reflect the developer himself. The reason for this is because it makes bias, lock-in syndrome, and other implicit influences on the outputs of our tech more explicit. It points the finger to what you should be analyzing, rather than falling into the trap of assuming that the output of a digital product is mostly influenced by the user’s input, when in actuality, what the output reflects is already heavily embedded in the tool itself.

    The system I chose previously was the perception of time and understanding its effect on people. It looks at the stakeholders of who is controlling that perception, and some specific applications in certain urban settings.

    The authorship one has on their perception of time illustrates my authorship philosophy perfectly. The individual might assume they are fully “in charge” of their own “time”. Their productivity, movement in a city, and understanding of time passing seems to be in their hands. Once time is wasted or used ineffectively, the user will look at their own hands instead of looking at the world built around them.

    The biggest “lock-in” idea is the concept of time itself. once developed from sun-time, to water-time, to gravity-time, to spring-time, to now, quantum-time, is an untouchable, unmoving force that we live within. The way it presents itself in digital tools is the fact that time will always go back to Greenwich time, which is neither necessary nor accurate. In the tools I create, users will be more aware of what their perception of time stems from, but for this to be possible, their input is minimal.

    The copyright of the tool or AI is essential because it demands accountability. If people use ChatGPT as a search engine, then they will only have access to the world through the LLM’s lenses. Nothing the AI produces should be taken as an accurate representation of what exists in the world.

    Flexibility should include inputs, interfaces, languages, topics, strengths, and weaknesses, but not the content of the output.

    I should have the most control in my practice, and I should be clearly given credit for that, because whatever biases I am harboring, I should be held accountable for.

    Authors and artists should be credited, and the AI should also be credited for transparency and accountability. User input should be given out responsibility, with the understanding that more input leads to more general results.

    To apply this to broader tech, in general, it seems if the input by the user is less, the output will be dependent on the lock-in ideas that exist, so on the opinion of the internet. It will be more focused and more steeped in bias and stereotype. Some are explicit stereotypes, but the overwhelming majority are the implicit ones - bias towards a certain type of logic, or representation, or language, that we mistakenly accept as fact. The more input the user has, the technology is forced to generalize, so the bias is less influential.
