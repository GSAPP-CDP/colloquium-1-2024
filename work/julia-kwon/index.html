1 – Systems

Featured Image

An analysis of a complex world system.

What is the system?


A system that I am particularly drawn to is the parasitic system, such as mycelium. Mycelium, the vegetative part of fungi, consists of a network of fine white filaments (hyphae) that infiltrate and colonize host organisms. This system exemplifies fascinating dynamics and interactions within ecosystems, particularly through its parasitic relationships.

Whom does it impact?


In this system, the elements include the mycelium, the host organism (such as plants or trees), and the surrounding environment. The function of the parasitic mycelium is to extract nutrients from the host, often at the host's expense, to sustain its own growth and reproduction. The feedback loop in this system is intricate; as the mycelium extracts nutrients, it weakens the host, potentially leading to a reinforcing feedback loop where the declining health of the host further facilitates the spread of the mycelium.


Conversely, a balancing feedback loop may occur if the host develops defenses that limit the spread of the parasite, maintaining a dynamic equilibrium. Stocks in this system include the nutrients within the host and the biomass of the mycelium. Flows represent the transfer of these nutrients from the host to the mycelium. The frequency and delay of these flows depend on environmental conditions, such as moisture and temperature, which can accelerate or slow down the mycelium's growth. Leverage points in the parasitic mycelium system could involve altering environmental conditions to disrupt the parasitic relationship, such as changing soil composition or introducing competing organisms. The system's hierarchy is evident in the mycelium's position within the ecosystem, often acting as a decomposer but shifting roles when engaging in parasitism.

What are its leverage points?


Boundaries of this system are defined by the extent of the mycelium's reach and the host organism's range. The resilience of this system is notable; mycelium can persist in adverse conditions and quickly exploit opportunities for growth. This resilience allows the system to withstand disturbances, such as changes in environmental conditions or the introduction of new host defenses, and maintain its essential functions and structure. The most interesting dynamic of this system is the mycelium's ability to adapt and exploit its host, showcasing nature's intricate balance between cooperation and competition. However, this adaptability can also be problematic, as it can lead to the decline or death of host organisms, potentially disrupting entire ecosystems. Understanding and managing this system is crucial for maintaining ecological balance and health.

2 – Authorship

Recording and Collecting Recycling Behaviors in Apartment Buildings using Participatory Gadget (APP)

Useful Lock-In in My System

The ultimate author of my system is the City or the Municipality that controls the recycling efforts. Co-authors that also control my system are individual entities participating in recycling efforts laid out by the city. Within each apartment building, there are people who collect the recycling and place it outdoors for pickup. These entities responsible for sorting through the recycling are also co-authors. In my system, everyone, even those who do not participate in the recycling process, are authors. Everyone who lives in the apartment units is a participant in recycling, whether actively or passively.


Lock-in measures

Data Privacy_Ensuring that the data collected by the app does not tap into individuals' identification, addresses, or phone numbers. This is crucial to protect personal information and maintain trust. Compliance and Standards_Adhering to municipal guidelines and standards to ensure the system’s integrity and reliability.


Flexibility in My System

Flexibility is better used in Data Availability_Making the number of participants in each apartment building available as open-source data. This allows any audience to use the data for research or development purposes, fostering innovation and wider use. User Engagement_Providing a flexible system that can adapt to the various needs and preferences of users, encouraging broader participation and interaction.


Variability in User Interface Design

If there were an app that collects and tracks recycling behaviors of participants in apartment buildings, it could:

For Active Participants

Lock-In_securely record participation without compromising personal data. This would enhance user trust and compliance. Impact_Provides a clear and reliable map or graph of recycling participation, helping the city track and improve recycling efforts.

For Passive Participants

Flexibility_Simplified interface that allows easy reporting of recycling activities, perhaps with incentives for participation. Impact_Encourages more residents to become active participants, thereby increasing overall recycling rates.


Authorship Models and Credit

User Author_Residents and building staff who contribute data through the app should be credited for their participation. An incentive system, such as discounts on public transportation, could further motivate involvement. Program Author_As the developer, my role is to ensure the system is ethical, secure, and transparent. Masses Author_The city and its broader community, who provide the initial data and context, should be acknowledged for their foundational role.


Copyright Considerations

AI and Copyright_I believe enforcing copyright laws for AI might hinder the development of new tools. Instead, a reasonable tax or profit-sharing model, such as a percentage of profits going back to the municipality, could support public resources without stifling innovation. User-Generated Content_Users should retain rights to their contributions, promoting ownership and responsibility. Source Authors_Authors of original datasets should be acknowledged and compensated, ensuring fair use and ethical practices.


Responsibility and Accountability

Being an author, whether a user, developer, or data contributor, carries significant responsibility. Accountability should be maintained through: Transparency_Clear communication of data usage and decision-making processes. Ethical Guidelines_Establishing and adhering to standards that prevent misuse and harm. Legal Frameworks_Ensuring compliance with relevant laws and regulations.


Control and Power Distribution

Stakeholders with Control

City/Municipality_Should have control over the system’s overall direction and data security. Users_Should have control over their personal data and participation records. Developers_Need to maintain system integrity and ensure ethical standards are met.


Implementation

User Empowerment_Through privacy settings, customizable interfaces, and clear ownership policies. Developer Oversight_Via rigorous testing, ethical guidelines, and continuous monitoring. Author Compensation_By implementing frameworks for licensing and royalties.


Conclusion

Balancing “lock-in” and flexibility in technology design is essential to cater to the varied needs of stakeholders in the recycling system. Thoughtful approaches to authorship, responsibility, and control can create an ethical, efficient, and user-friendly environment that encourages participation and innovation in recycling efforts.

Products


1. Strava (Fitness Tracking)

Strava is a fitness tracking app that allows users to record their physical activities such as running and cycling. The app collects data on user activities, creates maps and graphs, and provides a social platform for users to share their achievements and progress.

Authorship Model

User Author: Users are the primary authors as they record and share their activities. Each user’s contribution is personalized and unique. Program Author: Strava developers create the algorithms and interfaces that collect, analyze, and display user data. Community Author: Aggregated data from all users contributes to community features such as leaderboards and heatmaps.

Why It Resonates

Strava’s model is similar to your recycling app in that it collects individual user data to create a larger community-based dataset. It respects user privacy while allowing users to contribute to a bigger picture. The app’s ability to incentivize participation through social sharing and gamification could be adapted to encourage recycling behavior.


2. Waze (Community-based Traffic and Navigation App)

Waze is a GPS navigation software that relies on community input to provide real-time traffic and road information. Users can report accidents, traffic jams, and other hazards, which are then shared with the community to improve navigation.

Authorship Model

User Author: Users contribute real-time data by reporting road conditions and traffic incidents. Program Author: Waze developers design the system that processes user reports and integrates them into the navigation algorithms. Community Author:The collective input from all users enhances the accuracy and utility of the app for everyone.

Why It Resonates

Waze’s community-driven data collection model is akin to your recycling app’s approach of using user input to track and improve recycling efforts. The app’s balance of user-contributed data and system-generated insights can help create a dynamic and responsive recycling tracking system.


3. Duolingo (Language Learning App)

Duolingo is a language learning platform that uses gamification to engage users in learning new languages. The app tracks user progress, provides personalized feedback, and encourages practice through a series of interactive lessons and challenges.

Authorship Model

User Author_Users generate data by completing lessons, exercises, and tests, which the app tracks to personalize the learning experience. Program Author_Duolingo’s developers create the educational content, algorithms, and user interface that guide and assess user learning. Community Author_The collective data from all users helps refine the app’s content and features, benefiting the entire learning community.

Why It Resonates

Duolingo’s model of tracking individual user progress and using that data to improve the overall learning experience parallels your recycling app’s goal of tracking individual recycling behavior to enhance community recycling efforts. The app’s use of gamification to motivate users can also be applied to encourage recycling participation.


Generative Zine



Authorship Philosophy

Rooted in the notion of collective exchange of information, this bespoke game of participation simulates synergy created from collaboration. When the participant accepts further "meetings" of another players, he/she will be able to acquire more data from the pool. If the player does not wish to meet other players, he/she will have ended the game without much additional data being collected. Each player leaves a trace of the game, where a small thread of data will be attached with his/her character. From this generation of thread, the data pool will select a thread matching and an outcome of a combination of data will be given in a short narrative. The takeawy from this game is the philosophy that the synergy created from the collective data pool is more powerful than a single participant experience.

Data Manifesto

In my future practice, the types of data and collection methods that resonate most involve ecological, spatial, and observational data. Each dataset offers unique affordances.

Ecological Data

Ecological data illuminates species interactions and environmental conditions, supporting my hypothesis about mycelium systems. Finding relevant ecological data was challenging but essential for validating my arguments.

Spatial Data

Spatial data, collected via GeoJSON and mapping tools, captures precise locations and distributions of mushrooms and plants. It paints a clear picture of spatial relationships but often lacks detailed contextual data. This data can have politics, influenced by the collectors' objectives, potentially impacting accuracy.

Observational Data

Observational data from field studies provides a first-person narrative view, offering granular insights but may be biased by the observer's perspective. These datasets together illustrate complex ecological systems but leave out nuanced behavioral patterns. Concerns about gaps in data can be addressed by creating custom datasets through user interviews, surveys, and deploying sensors to capture real-time data, ensuring a comprehensive understanding.
